Wisconsin Card Sorting Task Models

BACKGROUND        Main research question (RQ): how do neural correlates of learning differ between levels of performance, in a dynamic decision-making task under uncertainty?

We aim to depart from more traditional ideas of comparing dichotomised average neural data, and instead examine individual neural responses (or predicates) to individual learning events, and develop profiles of these relationships (how they evolve over time).

We use Wisconsin Card Sorting Task to probe learning under uncertainty. This instrumental learning task provides several nice features, elaborated below, and a rich literature. To understand learning events, we interpret learning in terms of reinforcement learning (RL) and predictive processing (PP). RL can provide a framework to interpret observations across the task or task-phases, while PP can allow interpretation of trial-wise observations. Neural correlates of learning include theta- and alpha-band phase and amplitude in the fronto-parietal network (indexing attention and related to RL) and ERPs from the P3 complex (indexing belief updating and related to PP).

METHODS                Obtain behavioural data from two versions of a card-sorting task:

  1. traditional symbolic 3-matching rule version of Wisconsin Card Sorting Task (hereafter called WCST, see Figure 1 below),
  2. novel rich-stimuli 4-matching rule version called Wisconsin-ish Global-Local Dissociation task (called WishGLD, see Figure 2 below); also provides EEG+EOG

Adapt a Q-learning model from van Slooten et al (2018, 2019) to fit behavioural data from WCST and WishGLD (see Figure 3 below). Fit model parameters to individual subjects’ data; parameters are: learning rates αgain, αloss (encoding positive and negative rewards), and β (encoding sensitivity to value and thus exploratory vs exploitary behaviour). Relating these parameters to individual learning events, i.e. trials, could be too speculative (but see ibid), but card sorting tasks provide phases to which parameters can quite clearly relate. The search phase involves negative feedback and exploration, while the category learning phase involves positive feedback and exploitation. Oscillatory EEG could be related.

Finally, examine trial-wise EEG P3 complexes (feedback-locked P3a,b and target-locked P3b) to track how they change over task phases and whole task duration. Barceló (2020) has described how card sorting tasks fit neatly within a PP framework (see Figure 4 below), since feedback can be viewed as predictive cues updating posterior belief about the task’s matching rule hidden state, and thus setting subsequent response selection. It is not yet clear whether we should also aim to build a PP generative model of card sorting, to provide behaviourally-fitted parameters to relate to P3s; it might be enough to observe the patterns of P3s predicted by the PP approach (e.g. in Barceló, 2020).

OUTCOME                We expect to show the patterns of change of neural correlates which lead to better or worse performance, in terms of error and RT minimisation; and further show how learning model parameters help explain those patterns.

Study task 1: WCST

WCST combines several features that are helpful:

  • Trial-based (simpler to define behavioural and neural variables)
  • Clear structure to trials: cue - choice - feedback
  • Series of trials => explore-exploit phases, based on matching-rule changes
  • We know explicitly the hidden states (matching-rules) which subjects must learn
  • Though there is no trend or ‘narrative’ to learn over the task duration, nevertheless subjects can learn to perform with minimal errors; this is termed ‘learning to learn’
  • Probabilities of reward are absolute (choice of correct matching rule has 100% chance of reward), yet change often enough with enough alternatives to induce complex cognition and neural processing (which we can track)
  • Task demands are linked to subject’s internal model of statistical structure & sensorimotor contingencies

Study task 2: WishGLD

Figure 2. A schematic of a single trial. Numpad shown for illustrative purposes. Matching rule for the task shown is global object (face).

WishGLD (Cowley & Lukander, 2016) swaps symbol features for hierarchical stimuli with: global object, global dominant color, local object, local object orientation. There are thus 4 matching rules. Also, stimuli are in two variants: faces made of pointer objects, and letters made of (small) letters. ‘Stay’ trials are set to 5-7 correct repetitions.

WishGLD provides an overall more complex dataset to test our approach:

  • More complex space of hidden matching-rules to explore
  • More complex stimuli (however, all stimuli are equal in perceptual statistical terms)
  • Global vs Local matching rules correspond to different attention ‘modes’, visible in EEG
  • Two stimuli variants allow test of percept processing influence on learning
  • Dataset also contains control conditions using only 2 matching rules, i.e. no inference

RL framework & model

van Slooten, et al (2018, 2019) used RL to model learning in a value-based decision making task, fitting parameters of a Bayesian hierarchical Q-learning model to behavioural data and then comparing trial-wise parameters (van Slooten, et al, 2018) to pupillometry, and group-level parameter variation to spontaneous blink rate (van Slooten, et al, 2019). Others have done similar work with respect to EEG (Humann, et al, 2020), however the RL model of van Slooten seems ideal for WCST since it employs two learning rates.

Figure 3. Graphical representation of the hierarchical Bayesian Q-learning model. The inner plane represents within-subject trial-by-trial RL behaviour. Variables ri (t-1) (outcome for participant i on trial t-1) and chi(t) (choice of participant i on trial t) were obtained from the behavioural data.  The outer plane represents per-participant parameter estimates:

αGi (αGain participant i),

αLi (αLoss participant i), and βi (β participant i),

that were fit separately for participants in the low and high sEBR group. Per-participant parameter estimates were modelled using a probit transform

z’i (α'Gi, α'Li,  β'i).  

z’i were drawn from group-level normal distributions with mean μz' and standard deviation σz'. The outermost layer represents group-level mean and standard deviations of the Q-learning model parameters. A normal prior was assigned to all group-level means, μz' ~ N(0,1), and a half-Cauchy prior to all group-level standard deviations, σz' ~ Cauchy(0,5). A weakly informative prior such as this is recommended in small sample sizes to reduce the influence of the priors on posterior distributions. Shaded variables are obtained from the behavioural data and used to fit the model. Diamond shaped nodes are deterministic, as they are derived from the model fit. Circular unshaded nodes indicate continuous variables. Arrows indicate dependencies between variables. Φ() represents the probit transform.

From van Slooten, et al,  2018

PP framework

In PP, action and perception are cyclically bound together by the imperative to minimise uncertainty (the surprise expected following an action). Thus, stimulus qualities become relevant because sensorimotor responses are part of the cycle, and task (matching rule) interacts with stimulus (color, shape, number are processed at different neural levels) in a non-linear way. Also then, card sorting task phases correspond to inference, and learning of perceptual categories. The expected pattern of P3 is different between phases, and evolves throughout the phase (Figure 4); while at the whole-task level we also expect to see the P3s evolving to represent learning to learn, i.e. a measure of task efficiency. D’Alessandro, et al, (2020) described a ‘Bayesian Brain’ model of WCST.

WCST trial structure

The rule repetition should be long enough to allow for both searching and reinforcing phases: but we don’t track hits vs misses, so it can’t work responsively (maybe this is why the demonstrator uses 10 reps per rule). Let’s try a pseudo-responsive structure, aiming to track learning by decreasing trial count progressively, e.g.

10 > 9 > 8 > 8 > 7 > 7 > 7 > 6 > 6 > 6 > 6 > 5 > 5 > 5 > 5 (= 100 trials)

        where > denotes rule change

A variant

The 7s, 6s, 5s, could be mixed to make rule changes unpredictable, e.g.

Finally, the big issue is rule overlap: often it seems the vanilla demo version of PsyToolkit has not controlled for the possibility of seeing the target card match one reference card by more than one rule. If this occurs during search, it makes it ambiguous what the rule is, even if you get positive feedback. To prevent this, target cards should be curated to exclude those that match on more than one rule. We have:

  • matching rules (C, N, S)
  • Reference card set R (circle1red, triangle2green, cross3blue, star4yellow)
  • Target card set T, selected where only 1 matching rule applies in any element of R (e.g. triangle3red, but not triangle1red as it matches circle1red in C,N)

This should be quite tractable to code, but is easy to mess up doing it manually, as I think the demo version shows.

mTurk

PsyToolkit is set up to integrate with mTurk, and has instructions here.

The paradigm for running your own apps on mTurk is to host the app outside mTurk (basically anywhere on the Internetz that is accessible for the workers), and then deploy to

mTurk only a simple "questionnaire". This first forwards the worker to your app (where they do the actual work), and at the end the app gives a secret-code (if everything was done properly) that the worker enters into the questionnaire to indicate that they've completed their task.

On mTurk, especially since our tasks are a bit complex, we have to be really careful to describe them accurately, and be prepared to answer questions the workers may have.

We need to first do a "dry-run" first to check that the instructions are okay.

Tim Brady has a tutorial here.
